How it works

The problems that came up building the Pips Solver, and how each was solved — the image parser first, then the energy-model solver, then getting it all to run in a browser.

← the auto-solver · be the solver yourself →

The challenge

A NYT Pips puzzle is an irregular board of cells grouped into coloured regions, each carrying a constraint (a target sum, <N/>N, “all equal” =, or “all different” ≠). You must tile every cell with a given multiset of dominoes so all constraints hold. Two distinct problems: read the puzzle out of a screenshot, and solve the constraint problem. The parser turned out to be the harder half. No machine learning and no network calls are used — just classical computer vision.

Part 1 · Parsing the screenshot

Finding the cell grid

Problem: cells sit on a square lattice, but the pixel size of a cell differs between screenshots, and the board is an arbitrary polyomino, not a full rectangle.

Solution: the cell pitch is recovered from the periodic dip in brightness at the thin gaps between cells — an autocorrelation of that signal gives the period regardless of image scale. The lattice phase is then locked by sliding the grid and maximising how much solid cell-tile it covers, which works on any polyomino shape.

Cell vs. hole vs. background

Problem: a lattice slot may be a coloured region cell, a neutral “free” cell, an empty hole, or just the beige board frame. The free-cell beige and the frame beige differ by only ~14 in RGB, so a colour threshold was hopelessly fragile (it kept dropping free cells or inventing phantom ones).

Solution: stop using a background colour at all. A real cell is a large, uniform tile that fills its slot; a non-cell is white, or a thin strip of frame. So a slot is a cell iff a single dominant colour covers most of its area — a structural test that treats free and region cells identically and needs no fragile colour reference.

Disconnected boards (the hard puzzles)

Problem: hard puzzles are several separate board pieces scattered across the canvas, and the pieces are not aligned to one shared lattice — a single global grid missed most cells.

Solution: split the board into connected components and fit a lattice phase to each piece independently (the pitch is global, the phase is per-piece). Slot sampling is restricted to the piece being processed so a tight gap between two pieces can’t bleed one into the other. Each piece gets its own coordinate band so dominoes can never wrongly bridge a gap.

A subtle case: two pieces sometimes share a single rounded container drawn around both, with only a thin beige rim joining them — visually disconnected but pixel-connected. Eroding the board mask by ~12 px before labelling strips that thin rim (and the thin inter-cell gaps), so cluster IDs always come from a cell-bearing core. A cell tile is ~80 px on a side and easily survives the erosion.

Phone screenshots wrap the puzzle in app chrome

Problem: a phone screenshot is not just the puzzle — it’s a status bar, a browser header, the puzzle, a URL bar, app icons, etc., all stacked vertically. Tile-like UI elements (a Photos thumbnail strip, a URL pill) look superficially like dominoes; at the smaller mobile rendering scale a constraint tag can also become pixel-connected to its region’s dashed border, gluing several tags into one super-blob.

Solution: a one-shot puzzle locator runs first: it finds the warm-beige board container, then searches below it for a band of grey-scale tile components with the right ~2:1 domino aspect ratio, taking just the first band so app toolbars and thumbnails below the tray are left out. The image is cropped to that bounding box before the rest of the pipeline runs. When a saturated tag-detection blob exceeds a single tag’s area, it’s split via closing-then-erosion so each tag is recovered as its own seed, dilated back inside its own bbox so it can’t bleed into a merged neighbour. All remaining pixel thresholds (tag area, pip area, erosion radius, tile area) scale with the detected cell pitch, so the same code handles desktop pitches around 100 px and phone pitches that can drop below 60 px.

The same approach also forces the pitch-detection autocorrelation to prefer the fundamental period rather than a harmonic: the smallest lag whose autocorrelation is within 80% of the peak wins, so the parser doesn’t accidentally lock onto 2× the true pitch on puzzles where every other row happens to look similar.

Which cells form a region

Problem: clustering every cell colour globally merged genuinely separate regions that happened to share a hue (e.g. three different purple regions on one board).

Solution: a region is a connected component of equal-colour cells on the lattice. Same-coloured regions that are spatially apart stay distinct, which is exactly the game’s own rule.

Matching a constraint tag to its region

Problem: the little constraint markers are very saturated, while a region’s fill is a pale, desaturated tint — matching by raw colour failed, and several regions can share a tag colour. Nearest-cell matching swapped tags between neighbouring regions.

Solution: a region’s fill is its tag colour alpha-blended over beige, so the fill lies on the line segment between “beige” and “tag colour”. Tags are assigned to regions by an optimal (Hungarian) assignment on a cost that combines that colour-blend residual with spatial proximity: colour separates differently-hued neighbours, proximity separates regions that share a colour.

Reading the constraint text

Problem: the white glyph (a number, or =/≠/</>) is punched out of the solid tag, and small built-in OCR was unreliable on it. The = symbol’s bars even reach the tag edge, defeating naive flood-fill isolation.

Solution: recover the solid tag shape by morphologically closing its colour mask (this re-seals the punched text), and the glyph is then the bright pixels inside it. Split that into characters by column gaps and classify each against a small template library built once from real screenshots (tools/build_templates.py). New glyphs — >, the digit 0 from a “>0” tag, multi-digit numbers like 11/14 — were added by labelling them from new screenshots as they appeared.

Part 2 · Solving with an energy model

Problem: a brute-force tiling search is astronomically large, yet the answer must be exact — a “nearly right” board is wrong.

Solution: every region constraint contributes an energy that is zero exactly when satisfied and grows with the distance to satisfaction (see Constraint.violation). A valid solution is an assignment of total energy 0. The solver walks domino tilings depth-first, but for each region it keeps an admissible lower bound on the energy still reachable given the cells already filled (a partly-filled sum region can only land in a known window of totals; an “all-equal” / “all-different” region can only get worse). The branch is pruned the instant any region can no longer reach 0. That makes the search both exact and fast — milliseconds even for the 28-cell hard board.

Because a found solution is only as trustworthy as the parse, verify_solution independently re-checks the result from scratch: every cell covered exactly once, each pair of cells orthogonally adjacent, the domino multiset used exactly, and every constraint satisfied at energy 0. Nothing is reported as “solved” unless it passes that.

What “backtracking with admissible pruning” actually does

The solver is doing a depth-first traversal of a search tree. The root is an empty board; each node is a partial board state; each edge is “try this domino in this orientation, with these two cells”; a leaf is either a fully-tiled board (a candidate solution) or a dead end. Naively this tree is astronomical — for a 28-cell hard puzzle the unrestricted branching factor is in the dozens at every step, so the leaves number in the trillions.

What keeps it tractable is the per-region energy bound: every time a domino is placed, each touched region’s lower bound on its eventual energy is recomputed; if that bound is already above zero, the whole subtree below this node cannot reach energy 0 and we don’t explore it. For the easy puzzle pruning is so aggressive that no backtracking happens at all — the path is essentially straight. For the hard puzzles it’s the difference between “a few milliseconds” and “will not finish in the age of the universe.” The path the solver actually commits to is exactly the sequence of placements you see when you press Play on the solver view.

Part 3 · Running it in a browser

Problem: the parser/solver are Python (NumPy/SciPy/Pillow), but GitHub Pages only serves static files — no server to run Python on.

Solution: Pyodide runs CPython compiled to WebAssembly in the browser. The page loads NumPy, SciPy and Pillow, writes the unmodified pips package into the virtual filesystem, and calls it directly — so results are byte-identical to the command line. One sharp edge: GitHub Pages runs Jekyll, which silently drops files beginning with _ — including the glyph-template file. A .nojekyll marker disables that.

The solution is also drawn graphically as an SVG: region-tinted cells, with each domino as a rounded tile — blue and wide for horizontal, orange and tall for vertical — so the placement and orientation are obvious at a glance.

Part 4 · When the parser isn’t sure

Why a review step is needed

Problem: the classical-CV pipeline is right almost all the time, but at small renderings (a phone screenshot of a screenshot, say) a tag glyph can occasionally be mis-classified — the dashed boundaries are thicker relative to the character strokes, one bar of an = bleeds into the tag edge, and the runner-up template wins by a hair. Solving against a wrong constraint silently gives the wrong answer, with no signal to the user that anything is off.

Solution: the parser already carries soft signals at every stage; it just wasn’t surfacing them. The Help-Me mode keeps every per-item confidence score and, when any one of them falls below a threshold, the app pauses before solving and shows a Review panel where every parsed value — constraints, dominoes, even the cell-to-region assignments — is editable. The user confirms or corrects, hits Solve, and the corrected puzzle is solved by the same energy-model solver, bypassing the parser so the fixes always stick.

What “low confidence” means here

Each item gets its own kind of confidence signal — whatever the relevant stage of the pipeline already computes:

ItemSignal What it’s telling us
Region (constraint tag) best template match score, and the margin to the runner-up template how cleanly the glyph matched one character; a close runner-up means “could equally be that”
Region (matching) Hungarian assignment cost (colour-blend residual + spatial gap) how well this tag actually fits to its region by colour and proximity; a high cost suggests we ended up with the tag for the wrong region
Domino count of pip-blob candidates whose pixel area sits between half and one-and-a-half times the threshold any borderline blob means the parsed count could be off by one in either direction
Cell slot coverage: fraction of the lattice slot occupied by a single uniform tile colour coverage just above the existence threshold (0.60) is a cell only barely; might be a phantom slot near a board edge

Why these thresholds

The thresholds were not picked theoretically — they came from looking at the empirical distribution of each signal across all the puzzles in the test set, and choosing the gap between “every correctly-parsed item” and “the few known mis-reads”:

SignalThreshold Observed range (correct) Observed (mis-read)
glyph score< 0.85 0.92 – 1.00 0.732, 0.696
glyph margin to runner-up< 0.06 0.09 – 0.36 0.051, 0.015
tag–region cost> 32 typically 7 – 28 (no observed mis-match yet; bound set well above the highest correct value)
borderline pip blobs≥ 1 0 on every parsed domino in the test set any non-zero count means the count could be off
cell coverage< 0.70 0.85 – 1.00 on real cells just above the 0.60 existence cut-off is the danger zone

So each threshold sits in the empty band between “every correctly-parsed item” and any actual mis-read we’ve seen — nothing correct is ever flagged, and the two known mis-classified tags on the mobile screenshots (= read as sum=1; 1 read as ≠) are flagged loudly. If a future puzzle adds a new mis-read we’ll know exactly where in the table the new data point lands, and whether the bands need re-tuning.

What gets edited, and what re-solves

The review panel makes every parsed item editable, not just the flagged ones — the parser is occasionally confidently wrong, and the review step is the only place to fix that. Constraints get a kind dropdown plus a target field; dominoes have two pip inputs; each board cell is a coloured button, click cycles it through regions and right-click removes it from the board entirely. Pressing Solve hands the (possibly edited) structure to a separate webapi.solve_structured entry-point that rebuilds the puzzle from JSON and re-solves — the parser isn’t involved at all, so any correction the user makes is the final word.
